Modulus and Argument of Complex Numbers

Understanding polar form and geometric representation of complex numbers

üìö Introduction

Every complex number can be shown as a point on a graph. The modulus is how far that point is from the origin, like the length of a line from the center to the point. The argument is the angle that line makes with the x-axis (real axis) in radians.

Make sure your calculator is in Radian mode.
Look below. The modulus of $z$, $|z|$, is the hypotenuse of the triangle, with height of the imaginary part $bi$, and base of the real part $a$. Finding the modulus is just the hypotenuse.

üéØ Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you must be able to:

  • Calculate the modulus (absolute value) of a complex number
  • Find the argument (angle in radians) of a complex number
  • Know what each the Modulus and Argument is.

üîë Key Concepts

The Modulus

The modulus of a complex number represents its distance from the origin in the complex plane.

Modulus is like the hypotenuse of a right angle triangle with the real part as the base of the triangle, and the imaginary part as the height. It is denoted by $|z|$ and can be calculated using the formula:

üìñ Definition

Modulus: The distance from origin to complex number which is:

Let $z = a + bi$, the modulus of $z$ is $$|z| = |a + bi| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$$

The Argument

The argument of a complex number is the angle it makes with the positive real axis, measured counterclockwise, and in radians.
Its a key component to knowing where a complex number is on a diagram.

üìñ Definition

Argument: For a complex number $z = a + bi$, the argument is $\arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$ (in radians)

however, you must know what quadrant the complex number is in.

Here are the quadrant rules:
If $z = a + bi$:

  • Quadrant I (a > 0, b > 0): $\arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$
  • Quadrant II (a < 0, b > 0): $\arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right) + \pi$
  • Quadrant III (a < 0, b < 0): $\arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right) - \pi$
  • Quadrant IV (a > 0, b < 0): $\arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$
    • This means if the complex number is in quadrant II or III, you must add or subtract pi respectively.

    If the complex number is on an axis, then the argument is either 0, $\frac{\pi}{2}$, $\pi$, or $-\frac{\pi}{2}$ depending on which axis.

üìù Worked Examples

üìã Example 1: Finding the modulus and argument

Question: $z = 3 + 4i$. Find $|z|$ and $\arg(z)$.

Solution:
Step 1:

First lets find the modulus, $|z|$:

$$|z| = |3 + 4i| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$ So, the modulus of $z$, $|z| = 5$.
Step 2:

Now lets find the argument, $\arg(z)$:

$$\arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) \approx 0.93... \text{ radians}$$ Since 3 + 4i is in quadrant $I$, we do not need to add or subtract $\pi$.
Final Answer:

The modulus and argument of $z$ are:

$$\boxed{|z| = 5, \quad \quad \arg(z) \approx 0.93 \text{ radians}}$$

üìñ Extra Rules to Know:

Modulus and argument rules: if $z_1 = a + bi$ and $z_2 = c + di,\quad z_1, z_2 \in \mathbb{C}$, then:
the above just means: if $z_1$ and $z_2$ are complex numbers, then the following rules apply:

  • $|z_1 z_2| = |z_1| |z_2|$
  • $\left|\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right| = \frac{|z_1|}{|z_2|}$
  • $\arg(z_1 z_2) = \arg(z_1) + \arg(z_2)$
  • $\arg\left(\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right) = \arg(z_1) - \arg(z_2)$

üìù Harder Worked Examples

üìã Example 2: Finding the modulus and argument

Question: $z = 3 + 4i$ and $w = -5 + 10i$. Find $\quad$ $|wz|$ $\quad$and$\quad$ $\arg\left(\frac{w}{z}\right)$.

Solution:
Step 1:

Lets find $|z|$ and $|w|$:

$$|z| = |3 + 4i| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16}$$ $$|z| = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$ $|z| = 5$.

Now for $w$:

$$|w| = |-5 + 10i| = \sqrt{(-5)^2 + 10^2} = \sqrt{25 + 100} $$ $$ |w| = \sqrt{25 + 100} = \sqrt{125} = 5\sqrt{5}$$ $|w| = 5\sqrt{5}$.
Step 2:

Now lets find the argument for $z$ and $w$, $\arg(z)$ and $\arg(w)$:

$$\arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) \approx 0.93... \text{ radians}$$ Since 3 + 4i is in quadrant $I$, we do not need to add or subtract $\pi$.

$$\arg(w) = \arctan\left(\frac{10}{-5}\right) = \arctan(-2) \approx -1.11... \text{ radians}$$ Since -5 + 10i is in quadrant $II$, we must add $\pi$. $$\arg(w) = -1.11... + \pi \approx 2.03... \text{ radians}$$

üí° Tip

Remember $\rightarrow \arctan = \tan^{-1}$ on your calculator. Make sure use the first diagram to help you out. Click here to jump to it.
Make sure you're in radian mode

Final Answer:

Lets use our Mod and Arg rules to solve for $|wz|$ and $\arg\left(\frac{w}{z}\right)$:

Remember: $|z_1 z_2| = |z_1| \times |z_2|$, so: $$|wz| = |w| \times |z|$$ $$|w| \times |z| = 5\sqrt{5} \times 5 = \boxed{25\sqrt{5}}$$ Remember: $\arg\left(\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right) = \arg(z_1) - \arg(z_2)$ $$ \arg\left(\frac{w}{z}\right) = \arg(w) - \arg(z)$$ $$\arg(w) - \arg(z) \approx 2.03 - 0.93 = \boxed{1.1 \text{ radians}}$$

üö´ Mistake: Ignoring the Quadrant When Finding Argument

Many students use \( \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{b}{a} \right) \) to find the argument but forget to check the sign of the real and imaginary parts. This leads to incorrect angles for complex numbers in Quadrants II, III, and IV.

üëâ Always consider the signs of both real and imaginary parts before finalising the angle. You may need to add or subtract œÄ (or 180¬∞) to get the correct value.

üèÜ Challenge Exam Question

üìã Exam-style Question:

The complex numbers $z_1$ and $z_2$ are given by:
$z_1 = 2 - i$ and $z_2 = -8 + 9i$

(a) Plot $z_1$ and $z_2$ on a single diagram. (1 mark)

Find, showing your working:
(b) the value of $|z_1|$, (2 marks)
(c) the value of $\arg(z_1)$, giving your answer in radians to 2 decimal places, (2 marks)

üîç Show Solution
Solution:
Part (a):

Plot $z_1 = 2 - i$ at point (2, -1) and $z_2 = -8 + 9i$ at point (-8, 9) on a diagram.

Part (b):

Calculate the modulus of $z_1$:

$$|z_1| = |2 - i| = \sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 1} = \sqrt{5}$$
Part (c):

Calculate the argument of $z_1$:

$$\arg(z_1) = \arctan\left(\frac{-1}{2}\right) \approx -0.46 \text{ radians}$$ Since $z_1$ is in Quadrant IV, no adjustment needed.

üìã Summary

üéØ Key Takeaways

  • The modulus of a complex number $z = a + bi$ is $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$, representing its distance from the origin.
  • The argument of a complex number is the angle it makes with the positive real axis, found using $\arg(z) = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$ and adjusted for the correct quadrant.
  • Modulus and argument rules: $|z_1 z_2| = |z_1||z_2|$, $\arg(z_1 z_2) = \arg(z_1) + \arg(z_2)$, and similar for division.
